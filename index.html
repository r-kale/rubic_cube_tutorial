How to use this page

This page is two things in one: a working solver and a complete beginner's course for the 3×3 Rubik's cube. Everything runs in your browser from this single file — no internet needed once it's open.

  • Stuck on a real cube? Press Scramble a few times to see how solutions look, or copy your cube's twists onto the virtual cube with the move pad, then press Solve step-by-step and play the solution back at your own pace.
  • Want to learn? Read the steps below. Every algorithm on this page is a button — click it and the cube above performs it. Each step also has a Practice button that sets the cube up so only that step is left to do.
  • Keyboard: press U D L R F B to turn faces, hold Shift for the counter-clockwise (prime) version.

Foundations

Know your cube

A 3×3 cube has 26 visible pieces, and they are not all alike:

  • 6 centers — one colored sticker each. Centers never move relative to each other: the white center is always opposite yellow, green opposite blue, orange opposite red. A face's color is defined by its center.
  • 12 edges — two stickers each. An edge belongs between two specific centers.
  • 8 corners — three stickers each.

This is the single most useful mental shift for a beginner: you don't solve stickers, you solve pieces. The white-green edge piece has exactly one home: between the white and green centers, with each sticker touching its matching center.

Throughout this guide (and in the solver's solutions) we hold the cube the same way speedcubers do: white face down, yellow face up. The simulator starts with green facing you, orange on the right, red on the left, and blue at the back.

The language

Move notation

Every algorithm is written with six letters, one per face: Up, Down, Left, Right, Front, Back. A bare letter means: turn that face 90° clockwise, as if you were looking straight at that face. Two modifiers change the turn:

  • Prime (an apostrophe, said "R-prime"): counter-clockwise. R'
  • 2: a half turn (180°). R2

Step 1 of 7

The white cross

Goal, seen from below: white edges around the white center — and each edge's side color matching its center.

Find the four edges that have a white sticker and bring each one home: between the white center (bottom) and its matching side center. A correctly placed white-green edge shows white on the bottom face and green touching the green center.

How to place a white edge

  • Edge in the top layer, white facing up: turn U until the edge sits over its matching center (side sticker aligned with same-colored center), then turn that face twice (e.g. F2) to drop it into place.
  • Edge in the top layer, white facing sideways: the fastest fix is usually to steer the edge with the side faces; a reliable recipe when it's aligned over the front:
  • Edge in the middle layer: turn the adjacent side face to lift it into the top layer, undo any damage to already-placed edges, then use the cases above.
  • Edge in the bottom, but wrong or flipped: turn that face twice to send it up, then place it properly.

This step rewards intuition more than memorization — it's normal for it to feel slow at first. Watch how the solver does it: its cross solutions are short and never break edges it already placed.

Step 2 of 7

White corners — finish the first layer

Now insert the four white corners. Each goes between the white center and its two side colors — the white-green-orange corner belongs at the front-right-bottom, touching all three of those centers.

The one trick you need

  1. Find a white corner in the top layer. Turn U until it sits directly above its home slot (match its two non-white colors to the two side centers below it).
  2. Hold the cube so that slot is at the front-right, and repeat the sexy move until the corner drops in, white facing down. It always takes between 1 and 5 repetitions.
  3. If a white corner is stuck in the bottom layer in the wrong slot (or twisted), hold that slot at the front-right and do once to pop it into the top layer — then use steps 1–2.

When the step is done, the entire bottom layer is solid white with a matching first row of color all the way around.

Step 3 of 7

Middle layer edges

Four edges remain that have no white and no yellow: they belong in the middle layer. Find one in the top layer and look at its side sticker (the one facing outward, not up).

  1. Turn U until that side sticker sits over the center of the same color — the edge now forms an upside-down "T" with that face.
  2. Look at the sticker on top of the edge. It matches either the center to the right or to the left of the face you're looking at.

Goes left

Both algorithms are the same idea mirrored: dodge the edge away, bring its slot up, insert, restore. If a middle edge is stuck in a slot (wrong slot or flipped), perform the "goes right" algorithm for that slot with any top-layer edge — it kicks the stuck edge out — then insert it properly.

After this step, two entire layers are done. Only the yellow layer remains.

Step 4 of 7

The yellow cross

Goal, top face: a yellow plus sign. Side colors don't matter yet.

Ignore corners completely. Look only at the yellow edges on the top face — you'll have one of four patterns: a dot, an L (two adjacent), a line (two opposite), or the finished cross. One algorithm advances you along that chain:

  • Dot: do the algorithm from anywhere. You'll get an L.
  • L: turn U so the two yellow edges point up-and-left (9 o'clock and 12 o'clock), then do the algorithm. You'll get a line.
  • Line: turn U so the line is horizontal (left–right), then do the algorithm. Cross complete.

Step 5 of 7

Match the yellow edges to their centers

Your yellow cross is up, but the edges' side colors probably don't match the side centers. Turn U and see how many you can line up at once — usually two.

  • If two adjacent edges match: hold the matching pair at the back and right, then do the algorithm below — it swaps the front and left edges.
  • If two opposite edges match: do the algorithm once from anywhere, then you'll find an adjacent pair to finish with.

Don't worry that this scrambles the yellow corners — they're the next two steps.

Step 6 of 7

Send each yellow corner to its slot

Now put the four remaining corners in the right positions — twisted is fine, we only care where they are. A corner is "positioned" when its three sticker colors match the three faces meeting at its spot (in any rotation).

  1. Find a corner that's already positioned. Hold the cube so it's at the front-right-top.
  2. Do the cycling algorithm below — it spins the other three corners around while that one stays put. Check again; repeat once more if needed.
  3. No corner positioned at all? Do the algorithm once from anywhere, and one will be.

Step 7 of 7

Twist the last corners — and it's solved

Every corner is home; some are twisted. This final step looks alarming and works like magic:

  1. Hold the cube with a twisted corner at the front-right-top.
  2. Repeat (2 or 4 times) until that corner's yellow sticker faces up.
  3. Without rotating the whole cube, turn only U to bring the next twisted corner to the front-right-top, and repeat.
Hold your nerve: the bottom two layers will look destroyed while you do this. They are not. The damage cancels out exactly when the last corner is twisted — then one final U turn or two and the cube is solved.

Getting faster

Tips, and where to go next

  • Learn pieces, not patterns. Before every algorithm, say out loud which piece you're placing and where it must go. This is what makes the method stick.
  • Never rotate the whole cube mid-algorithm. Most beginner mistakes are grip changes halfway through a sequence.
  • Practice steps in isolation with the practice buttons above — ten minutes on the middle layer teaches more than an hour of full solves.
  • Use the solver as a coach: scramble, try a step yourself, then press Solve step-by-step and compare its moves for that step with yours.
  • When the 7 steps feel easy, the natural next method is CFOP: it merges steps 1–3 into intuitive "F2L" pairs and replaces steps 4–7 with bigger algorithm sets (OLL and PLL).

A realistic learning arc

  1. Day 1: cross + corners (steps 1–2) without help.
  2. Day 2–3: middle layer (step 3), the hardest habit to build.
  3. Day 4–7: the four yellow-layer steps; only three short algorithms plus the corner twist.
  4. Week 2: full solves under 5 minutes. From there, speed is just repetition.
